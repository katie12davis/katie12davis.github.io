Passport Power - is it all that important?

A passport is one of the most powerful and important documents that someone can own, which some individuals are willing
to spend up to $2.9 million dollars for a new one. A new citizenship and passport enables escape from war
and instability and eases work and travel.

But what does a powerful passport mean for the country named on its cover?

The aim of my project is to establish how important passport power is to a country, in particular to its economy and its political stability. Whilst there are a
multitude of factors I could have looked at, I have picked ones that can be logically connected with passport power and global mobility. I have used global data for this to enable the broadest investigation possible.









The world is clearly becoming more open and interconnected, but how is this beneficial.











The Economy

Whilst it varies at the top, higher scored countries have the strongest GDPs.
Whereas countries scoring 105 or below, predominantly have a GDP/capita less than $10,000.

The trends for international tourism and export dependency are similar, in that countries with a low score are have a
lower dependency on exports and lower tourism numbers, and those with a high score vary more.

When looking at less developed Regions, there is a stronger relationship between export dependency and passport power.
A strong passport can enable local entrepreneurs to more easily sell their goods abroad, which is less
likely to have an impact in already highly developed nations, but for less developed nations this could be a powerful change.

With international tourism, it seems a country with a stronger passport is more appealing to visit, likely because
passport agreements are often bilateral. So a higher score often means more passports can easily visit your country.
But a strong passport does not induce travel outwards.

I expected a stronger passport would correlate with high remittance dependency, in particular in LICs as it
eases mobility. However countries with strong passports are highly unlikely to rely largely on remittances,
and weak passports rely on them much more, especially in the Caribbean.
However in Africa there is a slight trend with some countries that a stronger
passport correlates with increased remittance reliance.








Political Stability

There is an incredibly strong correlation between passport scores and the peacefulness/fragility of a nation. It is likely that citizens
from more stable nations are seen as less of a threat, so are more likely to gain visa-free travel or visa on a arrival agreements
from other countries.

Ease of doing business measures regulations directly affecting businesses and it seems countries with more
powerful passports have more favourable regulations, likely because they are more outward looking and
friendly as a nation, so are more likely to focus on business, rather than corruption.




Overall it does seem that a strong passport is more beneficial than simply easing the process of going on holiday.
Especially for low income countries where it creates a better environment for growth, allowing a diversification of
income into exports and tourism.

Challenges in data cleaning/analysis

My map had the most issues. I inserted a year slider which would not work because I was using the topojson as my first data source,
and looking up passport data onto it which worked for only one year. By starting with passport data and looking up the topojson,
the slider worked. I found alpha3 using pycountry, and matched region_id with region names.

There were also issues presenting the score, because I wanted to show both score and rank but needed a score inverse so both
would work in the same direction with blue representing good. Calculating the inverse in vega did not work since 2007, 2009
and certain countries had 0 scores due to an API fault, so the inverse calculated as infinity breaking the map. So I created
a unique number for every row of data (year and country). Which I matched with the inverse score calculated elsewhere, where
score ‘0’s had been dropped so no infinity would be calculated. Whilst some data is now missing, this would have been the case
regardless.

World Bank data misses countries in recent years so I manually chose the most recent year with maximum country variation.
